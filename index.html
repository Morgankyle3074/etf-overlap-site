Business Problem

Investors frequently construct portfolios by combining ETFs based on fund labels, themes, or classifications. While ETFs are diversified individually, portfolios composed of multiple ETFs may not generate additive diversification benefits. Overlapping holdings can create duplicated economic exposure, resulting in hidden concentration risk.

This structural redundancy is particularly relevant in markets characterized by elevated capitalization concentration, where a relatively small subset of securities dominate index weighting. As ETFs increasingly inherit these weighting structures, portfolios that appear diversified at the fund level may exhibit significant concentration at the holdings level.

Project Thought Process

Traditional diversification analysis often evaluates portfolios at the fund level. This project reframes ETFs as weighted baskets of securities, enabling holdings-level evaluation of economic exposure duplication.

  • Goal: Measure diversification quality beyond ETF count.
  • Method: Weight-adjusted overlap analysis.
  • Risk Lens: Overlap + Concentration Metrics (HHI, Effective Holdings).
  • Outcome: Identify structural portfolio redundancy.

The findings demonstrate that diversification assumptions based solely on fund variety may be misleading. Structural weighting and overlap dynamics materially influence portfolio concentration and risk exposure.

Investor Takeaway: Diversification must be evaluated at the holdings level, not inferred from ETF labels.
