Business Question & Key Finding

How often does “ETF stacking” create real diversification versus duplicated economic exposure?

Business Problem

Investors often combine ETFs by fund label (growth, dividend, broad market) and assume diversification improves automatically. But ETFs frequently share many of the same underlying holdings—especially cap-weighted funds—creating hidden duplication of exposure.

What This Means

The big takeaway: count ETFs less—analyze holdings more.

Project Thought Process

ETFs are diversified individually, but when you combine multiple ETFs, diversification must be evaluated at the holdings level. This project reframes each ETF as a weighted basket of securities and measures overlap using weight-adjusted intersection to capture true economic redundancy.

The results show overlap is structurally embedded in cap-weighted index construction. Some combinations (especially SPY + VOO) provide limited incremental diversification, while still increasing portfolio complexity. Weighting methodology influences risk: concentrated top weights can dominate portfolio behavior even if total holding count is high.
